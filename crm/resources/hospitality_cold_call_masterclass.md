## 1. THE MINDSET

**You are not interrupting them. You are rescuing them.**

Every property without a website is bleeding money to OTAs (Online Travel Agencies — Booking, Airbnb, Expedia, Vrbo). Every day they delay, a competitor closes their booking. You are the cure.

**Three mental rules before every call:**

- **The sale happens before you dial.** Decide they are going to buy. If you sound uncertain, they hear it.
- **The "no" is not personal.** It's a "no" to a script, a tone, or bad timing. Try again, differently.
- **You are the prize.** They are lucky you called. Most owners don't even know what they're losing.

**Energy is currency.** A bored voice loses. Stand up. Smile while talking — they hear it. Speak from your diaphragm, not your throat. Call after coffee, never after lunch.

## 2. THE FIRST 12 SECONDS

The first 12 seconds decide the entire call. If you sound like a telemarketer, they hang up. If you sound like a peer with valuable info, they listen.

### ❌ Don't say:
- "How are you today?" *(Instant telemarketer flag.)*
- "Do you have a moment?" *(Gives them an out.)*
- "I'm calling from [Company]..." *(Nobody cares about you yet.)*
- "We help businesses like yours..." *(Vague, salesy, dead.)*

### ✅ Say this instead — the universal opener:

> **"Hi — quick reason for the call. I came across [Property Name] looking for places in [Area] and I noticed something that's costing you real money every week. I work with hotels and rentals on the booking side and I want to tell you what I saw. Two minutes — fair?"**

**Why this works:**
- "Quick reason for the call" → respects their time, signals brevity
- "Costing you real money every week" → loss-framed, urgent, specific
- "I work with hotels and rentals on the booking side" → peer language, not vendor language
- "Two minutes — fair?" → micro-commitment they can't easily refuse

### Variations by property type:

**For a hotel:**
> "Hi — I'll keep this quick. I was looking at boutique stays in [Area] and noticed something about [Hotel Name]'s booking setup that's costing you 18–25% on every reservation. Worth two minutes to hear?"

**For an Airbnb / vacation rental:**
> "Hi — I came across [Property Name] on Airbnb and noticed you don't have your own booking site. There's a specific reason that's costing you €200–€300 per booking and I want to show you the math. Two minutes?"

**For a long-term apartment:**
> "Hi — I came across your listing for [Apartment Name]. Quick question — are you currently getting most of your tenants from Spitogatos or word of mouth? Because there's a whole category of renters you're invisible to right now. Two minutes to hear how?"

## 3. DISCOVERY

After they say "okay, go ahead," **don't pitch yet**. Ask 2–3 sharp questions first. People buy more from those who listen than from those who talk.

### The 3 power questions (use all three, in order):

1. **"Right now, where do most of your bookings come from — Booking, Airbnb, walk-ins, repeat guests?"**
   - Tells you their dependency. Higher OTA % = bigger pain you can solve.

2. **"What's your average commission costing you per booking — roughly?"**
   - Forces them to say the number out loud. The pain becomes real to them.

3. **"If a guest wanted to book directly with you and skip Booking — could they today?"**
   - 95% will say no. This is the gap your website fills.

**Listen. Take notes. Repeat back their words.** When you pitch, use the exact phrases they used. "You said you're paying around 18% per booking — here's exactly how we fix that..."

## 4. PITCH FRAMEWORKS

Every pitch follows the same skeleton:
**PROBLEM → CONSEQUENCE → SOLUTION → PROOF → CALL TO ACTION**

### 4.1 HOTELS & BOUTIQUE SUITES

**Problem:**
> "Right now, every booking that comes through Booking.com or Expedia is costing you between 18% and 25%. On a €120 room, that's €25–30. On a 200-night occupancy year, that's anywhere from €5,000 to €12,000 leaving your pocket — every single year."

**Consequence:**
> "And here's the kicker — the guest already chose you. They saw your photos, read your reviews, picked your hotel. You've already done the marketing. Booking just sits between you and them and takes a tax. If they had a way to book directly, you'd keep 100%. You don't have that way today."

**Solution:**
> "What I do is build hotels their own direct-booking system. Your own website, your own calendar, your own check-out. Synced with your existing channel manager so you never get double-booked. Guests who'd otherwise book through Booking can now book through you — same price, but you keep all of it."

**Proof:**
> "I've done this for boutique hotels of your size. Most owners see the website pay for itself within 30–60 days, and then it's pure profit margin every booking after that. Forever."

**Call to action:**
> "Let me do this — give me 24 hours and I'll send you a free mockup of what your direct-booking site would look like, with your photos and your name. No commitment. If you like it, we talk numbers. If you don't, you've lost nothing. Fair deal?"

### 4.2 AIRBNBS & SHORT-TERM VACATION RENTALS

**Problem:**
> "Every guest you take through Airbnb costs you 15–20% in fees. On a villa renting at €1,500 a week, that's €225–€300 gone — per booking. Across a season, you're looking at thousands."

**Consequence:**
> "But the bigger problem isn't even the commission. It's that you don't own the relationship. When a guest checks out, Airbnb keeps their email. You can never email them again. You can't send them a 'come back next summer for 10% off' offer. You can't build repeat customers. Airbnb does. You're building Airbnb's business with your property."

**Solution:**
> "A direct-booking website with your villa flips that. Guests book through your site, you collect their email, you own the list. Next season, you email them — they book direct, no commission. Year after that, same thing. You go from renting space on Airbnb's platform to owning a business with a real customer base."

**Proof:**
> "Here's the math: a website pays for itself after just 5 to 10 direct bookings. Most owners hit that in the first 2 months. Everything after that is pure savings — usually thousands of euros per year, every year, indefinitely."

**Call to action:**
> "Send me one good photo of the villa. By tomorrow I'll have a mockup of your direct-booking site, with your property, your photos, your branding. You look at it, you tell me if it's a fit. Sound good?"

### 4.3 LONG-TERM APARTMENTS FOR RENT

**Problem:**
> "Most landlords think their tenants come from Spitogatos or Facebook groups. But the highest-paying tenants — corporate relocations, expats on long contracts, digital nomads on multi-month leases — they don't browse Spitogatos. They Google '[area] furnished apartment monthly' and they expect a real website with photos, prices, and a contact form. Right now, you don't show up for any of those people."

**Consequence:**
> "Those are the tenants who pay on time, stay longer, and don't haggle. And they're going to your competitors who do have a website. Every month without one, you're choosing lower-quality tenants by default."

**Solution:**
> "A simple website with all your apartments in one place — photos, monthly rates, what's included, a contact form — turns you from 'a guy with apartments' into 'a real rental business.' That's what corporate relocators trust. That's what the embassies refer their staff to. That's what gets you premium rent."

**Proof:**
> "I've built these for landlords with anywhere from 2 to 20 units. Within the first 3 months, they're getting inquiries from a class of tenants they couldn't even reach before — and they can be choosier about who they accept."

**Call to action:**
> "Let me do a free mockup — one page, all your apartments, professional. You see it before you decide anything. If it's not what you want, we walk. Fair?"

## 5. THE 7 TONES — WHEN AND HOW TO USE EACH

Reading the client and matching their energy is **the single biggest skill** that separates rookies from closers. Use the wrong tone and you lose the call in 30 seconds. Match the tone, and they trust you instantly.

Here are the 7 client types and the exact tone to deploy.

### 🅰️ Tone 1 — **THE WARM PEER** (default tone)
**Use when:** The client sounds friendly, curious, willing to talk. This is your default starting tone.

- **Pace:** Medium. Conversational.
- **Volume:** Normal. Slight smile in the voice.
- **Word choice:** Plain language. "We," "you," "let's." No jargon.
- **Energy:** Like you're chatting with a friend who owns a business.
- **Example line:** *"Listen, I get it — you've been in this game a long time, you know what works. I just want to show you one thing that might surprise you. Two minutes."*

### 🅱️ Tone 2 — **THE QUIET EXPERT** (use with skeptics)
**Use when:** The client is older, sounds experienced, asks tough questions, doesn't want to be sold.

- **Pace:** Slow. Deliberate. Pause more.
- **Volume:** Lower than normal. They lean in.
- **Word choice:** Specific. Use numbers. Avoid hype words like "amazing," "incredible," "transform."
- **Energy:** Calm authority. You don't need this sale. You're sharing information.
- **Example line:** *"I'll be honest with you. I'm not going to sell you anything today. I just want you to know what your options are. Whether you do it with me or someone else, the math doesn't change — Booking takes 20% and you can stop that."*

### 🅲 Tone 3 — **THE ENERGETIC FRIEND** (use with younger or hospitality-passionate owners)
**Use when:** The owner sounds upbeat, runs a trendy place, is passionate about their property.

- **Pace:** Faster. Snappy.
- **Volume:** Slightly louder, lots of vocal variety.
- **Word choice:** Visual. "Picture this," "imagine," "you're gonna love this."
- **Energy:** Excited about their property as if it were yours.
- **Example line:** *"Okay so I looked at your photos and your place is gorgeous — like, why are you hiding it on Airbnb? You should have your own site, your own brand, people Googling you by name. That's where you belong."*

### 🅳 Tone 4 — **THE DIRECT CLOSER** (use with busy executives / hotel managers)
**Use when:** The client is short on time, transactional, says "what do you want" or "get to the point."

- **Pace:** Fast and clean.
- **Volume:** Confident, no filler.
- **Word choice:** Numbers. Outcomes. Cut every adjective.
- **Energy:** Respect their time. No small talk.
- **Example line:** *"30 seconds. Booking takes 20%. Direct site cuts that to zero. Costs €X one-time, paid back inside 60 days. I'll send a mockup tomorrow. Yes or no?"*

### 🅴 Tone 5 — **THE CONSULTATIVE ADVISOR** (use with sophisticated owners)
**Use when:** The client clearly knows the industry, mentions OTA contracts, dynamic pricing, or channel managers.

- **Pace:** Measured.
- **Volume:** Calm, professional.
- **Word choice:** Industry terms used correctly. "Channel manager," "RevPAR," "ADR," "occupancy rate," "direct conversion."
- **Energy:** Peer-to-peer with a fellow professional. No condescension.
- **Example line:** *"You already know the game — Booking is great for fill, but every direct booking lifts your RevPAR and your margin. The question is just whether your direct funnel is working as hard as it could. Let me show you what a properly tuned direct channel looks like for a hotel your size."*

### 🅵 Tone 6 — **THE PATIENT TEACHER** (use with non-technical or older owners)
**Use when:** The owner says "I don't understand computers," "my son does that," or sounds confused.

- **Pace:** Slower. Repeat key phrases.
- **Volume:** Warm, never patronizing.
- **Word choice:** Simple, concrete metaphors. No tech words.
- **Energy:** Reassuring. You handle everything. They don't lift a finger.
- **Example line:** *"I'll explain it simply. Right now, when someone wants to stay at your place, they go through a middleman who keeps part of the money. A website is just a way to skip the middleman. I build it, I take care of it, you don't touch anything. You just receive the bookings."*

### 🅶 Tone 7 — **THE ASSUMPTIVE WINNER** (use to close)
**Use when:** They've shown buying signals (asked about price, timing, what's included). Time to close.

- **Pace:** Confident, forward.
- **Volume:** Steady, slightly louder than Tone 1.
- **Word choice:** Use "when" not "if." "When we launch your site," "the first booking you get direct."
- **Energy:** It's already happening. You're just confirming details.
- **Example line:** *"Great. Here's what we'll do. I'll send the mockup tomorrow morning. We'll do a 15-minute call Wednesday to walk through it together. If everything looks right, we kick off Friday and you're live within 10 days. Does Wednesday at 11 work, or is afternoon better?"*

### 🎯 The Tone Switching Rule

Most calls start in **Tone 1 (Warm Peer)**, shift to **Tone 5 or 6** during pitch (depending on sophistication), and end in **Tone 7 (Assumptive Winner)** to close.

**Read the first 3 sentences of their reply** — it tells you which tone to lock into.

| What they say | Switch to tone |
|---|---|
| "Sure, I have a minute, what's up?" | Tone 1 → Tone 7 |
| "I've heard this pitch before." | Tone 2 (Quiet Expert) |
| "Yeah I love my place, what do you do?" | Tone 3 (Energetic Friend) |
| "Make it quick." | Tone 4 (Direct Closer) |
| "We use a channel manager already." | Tone 5 (Consultative Advisor) |
| "I don't really do computers." | Tone 6 (Patient Teacher) |
| "How much is it? When can we start?" | Tone 7 (Assumptive Winner) |

## 6. OBJECTION HANDLING

Every objection is one of 5 underlying fears. Identify the fear, address the fear, never argue.

### "I already use Booking.com / Airbnb."
> "Perfect — that means you already understand the booking world. A website doesn't replace those platforms. It runs alongside them. Booking still brings in the new guests who don't know you. The website captures the ones who already know you and would otherwise pay 20% extra to find you again. Best of both worlds."

### "I have Instagram / Facebook — that's enough."
> "Social media is rented land. Instagram changes its algorithm next month, your reach drops to zero overnight. A website is owned land — yours forever. It also shows up in Google, where 80% of bookings start. Instagram doesn't show up in Google searches. Different tools, both useful, but only one is permanent."

### "It's too expensive."
> "Compared to what? Compared to the €5,000 a year you're paying Booking? Compared to the guests who never find you because you're invisible on Google? A website costs less than the commission on 5 bookings — and then it works for years. The expensive option is the one you're doing right now."

### "I don't have time to manage a website."
> "You won't manage it. I do that. I build it, I host it, I keep it running. Calendar syncs automatically with your existing system. You only touch it if you want to. Most clients log in once a month, just to see how many bookings came in."

### "Let me think about it." / "Send me information by email."
> "Of course — but here's the thing. While you're thinking, your competitor with a website is taking the booking that would have been yours. Let me make it easy: I'll send you a free mockup tomorrow. You see exactly what your site would look like. No commitment, no pressure. If you don't love it, we never speak again. If you do, we keep going. Fair?"

### "I need to talk to my partner / spouse / accountant."
> "Smart — you should. To make that conversation easier, let me send you the mockup and a one-page breakdown of the numbers tonight. That way when you sit down with [partner], you have something concrete to look at, not just my pitch. What's the best email?"

### "We just don't need a website."
> "I hear you. Can I ask you one thing? When you're looking for a restaurant or a doctor or a hotel for yourself — what's the first thing you do? You Google it, right? Everyone does. The people booking your place do the exact same thing. They Google you. If you're not there, the next listing wins. That's all this is — making sure you're there when they look."

### "Send me an email and I'll look at it."
> "Will do — but emails get buried. Let me make this easier for both of us: I'll send the email AND I'll call you back Thursday at 11. Two minutes, you tell me if you opened it, if you have questions. Sound fair?"
*(Always anchor the next call. Never let an email be the end of the conversation.)*

### "I tried a website before, didn't work."
> "Tell me what you tried. *(Listen.)* Yeah — that's a common story. The reason it didn't work is because [it was templated / had no booking integration / wasn't optimized for Google / wasn't connected to a payment system]. What I do is different in [specific way]. The website is the easy part — the system behind it is what makes it actually generate bookings."

### "We're seasonal, only open 4 months a year."
> "Even better. Those 4 months are when you make your entire year. Every booking you take direct during that window saves you 20% — and that money compounds. Plus, the off-season is when guests plan and book for next summer. A website that's online 365 days catches those bookings even when your doors are closed."

## 7. THE CLOSE

Every great salesperson closes the same way: **assume the sale, give a choice between two yeses.** Never end on "do you want to buy?" — that's a yes/no question and yes/no questions get nos.

### The 6 Closing Plays

**1. The Mockup Close** *(softest, highest acceptance rate — use this 80% of the time)*
> "Let me build you a free mockup. Tomorrow morning you have it. You decide from there. Sound good?"

**2. The Calendar Close** *(when they're already warm)*
> "Let's lock in 15 minutes Wednesday to go through the mockup together. Morning or afternoon better?"

**3. The Two-Choice Close** *(when they're decided but stalling on details)*
> "We can do the standard package or the premium with online payments built in. Which one fits better for you?"

**4. The Urgency Close** *(when there's a real deadline)*
> "I'm taking on 3 more clients before [season/month]. If we start this week, you're live before [date]. After that, the next slot is in 6 weeks. Want to lock in?"

**5. The Risk-Reversal Close** *(when they're scared of commitment)*
> "Look — if you don't love the finished site, you don't pay. That's how confident I am. Worst case for you is some time on a phone call. Best case is a real direct-booking system that pays for itself by the end of summer. Want to do this?"

**6. The Walk-Away Close** *(when they're too cold and you need to flip the dynamic)*
> "Honestly, I don't think this is the right fit for you yet. Stick with Booking — it's working for you right now. If your situation changes and you want to capture some of that 20% you're paying them, my number stays the same. Take care."
> *(8 out of 10 times, this is when they say "wait, hold on, tell me more." Walking away signals you don't need them. Need is repulsive in sales.)*

## 8. THE FOLLOW-UP

**80% of sales happen on the 3rd to 7th touch.** If you give up after one call, you're leaving 80% of your money on the table.

### The 5-Touch Sequence (over 14 days)

| Day | Touch | Channel | What to send |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Call #1 | Phone | The pitch. Schedule mockup. |
| 2 | Mockup | Email | Subject: "Mockup of [Property] — direct booking site". One image, one paragraph, one question. |
| 4 | Call #2 | Phone | "Did you have a chance to look? What stood out?" |
| 8 | Value | Email or WhatsApp | Send a relevant case study or one-line tip. NOT a pitch. Build relationship. |
| 14 | Call #3 | Phone | The "deciding moment" call. "Want to lock this in or should I close the file?" |

### Email follow-up template (Day 2 — Mockup Send)

> **Subject:** Mockup of [Property Name] — direct booking site
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Quick mockup of what your direct-booking site could look like. This took me about an hour. The full version syncs with your booking calendar so you never get double-booked.
>
> [Image / Link]
>
> One question: which is more important to you right now — saving on commission, or showing up better in Google search? That changes which features I'd prioritize.
>
> Will call you Thursday around 11.
>
> [Your name]

### WhatsApp follow-up (more casual, often better with vacation rental owners)

> "Hey [Name] — sent the mockup over. Take a look when you get a sec. Curious what you think 👌"

## 9. VOICEMAIL SCRIPTS

If they don't answer, leave **a short, curiosity-driven message**. Most cold-call voicemails are 45+ seconds and get deleted instantly. Yours should be 15 seconds max.

### Voicemail #1 — First attempt
> "Hi [Name], it's [Your name]. I had a quick question about [Property Name]'s booking setup — I think there's a way you're losing about 20% you don't need to lose. I'll try you again tomorrow. Otherwise call me back at [number]. Thanks."

### Voicemail #2 — Second attempt (3 days later)
> "Hi [Name], [Your name] again. Still trying to reach you about [Property Name]. I won't keep calling — I'll try one more time Monday. If it's better timing, just text me at [number]. Talk soon."

### Voicemail #3 — Final attempt (1 week later)
> "Hi [Name], [Your name]. Last try. If now isn't the right time, no problem. If your situation changes, my number's [number]. Take care."

*(The "I won't keep calling" line is psychological reverse-pressure. They feel they should call back to not lose the opportunity. Works ~30% of the time.)*

| Airbnb host fee | 14–20% | Fact. Universal pain. |

> "Industry average for hotels is to do 25–40% of bookings direct. Most properties I see your size are doing under 10%. That gap is pure money on the table."

## 11. DAILY DISCIPLINE

The difference between rookies and top closers is **not talent — it's volume and consistency.** Here's the daily structure of a top hospitality cold caller.

### The Top Closer's Day

- **8:30–9:00am** — Coffee, stretch, review yesterday's notes. No phone yet.
- **9:00–11:30am** — **POWER BLOCK 1.** No emails, no WhatsApp, no breaks. Phone only. Target: 30 dials.
- **11:30–12:00pm** — Email follow-ups, mockup sends.
- **12:00–1:00pm** — Lunch. Outside if possible.
- **1:00–3:30pm** — **POWER BLOCK 2.** Target: 30 more dials.
- **3:30–4:30pm** — Admin: log every call, update CRM, schedule callbacks.
- **4:30–5:00pm** — Mockup work for tomorrow.
- **End of day** — Review: how many calls? How many connects? How many mockups sent? How many closes?

### The Numbers That Make a Closer

A trained hospitality cold caller, calling 60 dials a day, will average:
- **15–20 connects per day**
- **5–8 real conversations per day**
- **2–3 mockups sent per day**
- **0.5–1 close per day** = **10–20 closes per month**

If you're not hitting these — you're not making enough calls or not following the framework. **The framework works. Trust it. Run the reps.**

### The 3 Daily Mantras

1. **"Every no gets me closer to the next yes."**
2. **"My job isn't to convince anyone. It's to find the ones who are ready."**
3. **"The phone is a money printer. I just have to dial."**
